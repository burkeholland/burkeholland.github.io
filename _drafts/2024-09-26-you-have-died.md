It seems to be the case that everyone has some great _chronic_ struggle in their lifetime. Something that robs them of their sleep and joy. Something self-inflicted. I don't mean that self-inflicted in the sense that they are "getting what they deserve". I imagine most people are victims of something they had no control over. But they almost certainly blame themselves and their present suffering is self-inflicted.

I know this has been the case for myself. I have struggled with anxiety most of my life. One of my earliest memories is being scared to death that the earth would shrink up and dissapear. I could not have been more than 3 or 4. I also distinctly remember my mother taking me to talk to someone about it because I was so worried. That person did not help. They told me it might be possible if all water dissapeared off the earth. The whole thing was rediculous, but I must have suffered greatly with that worry because it's scalded into my mind.

Another early memory I have - I distinctly remember it being 1st grade - was that I was dying from internal bleeding. I don't remember how I learned about the subject to begin with, but I remember being terrified. Again, this must have been intense because I can see 1st grade Burke just as clearly today. 

Unfortunately, my 46 years thus far on the earth are full of these anecdotes. 

I didn't ask for a brain that works this way. I am fully aware that I often live in a distorted reality that I have constructed out of thin air. And yet, I still find myself trapped there much of the time. 

Those who don't suffer with this kind of affliction will find it...crazy. This is understandable. Why would someone create a world of suffering that does not exist and then choose to live in it? Why would you choose to sit in darkness all the time when all you have to do is walk outside and stand in the warm sunshine? It is a rare gift to just be alive.

I don't know what your struggle is. There is a good chance I wouldn't understand it anymore than you can understand mine. But I know that it's very real. I know that it's a weight that makes it hard just to get out of bed. To work. To be a good parent. An invisible burden that you carry everywhere you go.

Both you and I have the option to set the burden down and just leave it right where it falls - along the side of the road. But we cannot do that on our own because we don't know how to take the weight off. We cannot find the mechanism to release it. And in a way, we don't know how to live without it, so there is a good chance we would go back out to find it so we can pick it right back up again.

The history of humanity is a desperate cosmic search for help. A cry to the void for something bigger than ourselves that rescue us from ourselves. When people talk about a "god shaped hole in every heart", this is what they mean. 

Life is a series of things that we simply cannot control. The sickness, the betrayal, the failure, the constant rejection, the disgrace and the shame. Despite the articles that tell you how to avoid cancer, you have little control over whether or not you die young. Despite how good a partner you are, you have little control over whether the love of your life decides they prefer someone else. Despite all the podcasts that tell you to get to the gym and get your life in order, you have no actual control over whether or not you spend your life alone with an ache in your heart that makes it hard to even breathe.

In Collosians, Paul says to "Set your mind on things above, not earthly things." And in the very next sentence he says, "because you have **died** and your life is now hidden with Christ in God."

The problem is that my eyes are set on earthly things. But its all I have ever known. How can I set my mind on something I have never seen or held? This is the puzzle that we must solve. We have to die so that we can live.
